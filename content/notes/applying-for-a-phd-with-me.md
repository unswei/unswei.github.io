I regularly receive enquiries from students who would like to do a PhD in robotics, machine learning, autonomous systems, or related areas. I am always happy to hear from strong applicants, but it helps to understand how PhD admission and funding work in Australia.

In Australia, a PhD is usually not advertised as a fixed employment position in the same way it often is in Germany or parts of Europe. In Germany, for example, many PhD candidates are hired as research staff on a specific funded project. In Australia, the more common route is that a prospective student applies for admission to a higher degree research programme and separately applies, or is considered, for a scholarship.

At UNSW, and most other Australian universities, applicants normally need to identify a potential supervisor, discuss a possible research direction, prepare a research description, and then submit a formal application through the university system. UNSW describes this as part of the [Higher Degree Research application process](https://www.unsw.edu.au/research/hdr/application). Only complete applications are assessed.

For international applicants, funding is often the main practical issue. A strong academic record is important, but it does not automatically mean that a funded PhD place is available. UNSW has scholarships funded by the Australian Government, the university, faculties, schools, supervisors, industry partners, and international partners. Some scholarships cover tuition fees and provide a living stipend, but these are competitive. UNSW's [HDR scholarship information](https://www.unsw.edu.au/research/hdr/scholarships) and [scholarship guidelines](https://www.unsw.edu.au/content/dam/pdfs/research/higher-degree-research/hdr-scholarship-guidelines.pdf) describe different arrangements, including Research Training Program scholarships, University International Postgraduate Awards, tuition fee scholarships, and externally supported scholarships.

This means that "Do you have an open PhD position?" is not always the right question in the Australian system. You can get accepted for a PhD programme without scholarship, but then you need to pay fees. Sometimes I may have a funded project with a specific topic. More often, the question is whether there is a good research fit, whether the applicant is competitive for admission and scholarship, and whether a viable funding route exists.

If you contact me about a possible PhD, please send a short, specific email. It should include:

- your academic background and grades
- your research experience, especially thesis or publication work
- the area you want to work in
- why you think my group is a good fit
- whether you already have funding, are applying for external funding, or need a UNSW scholarship
- a CV, transcript, and, if available, a thesis abstract or writing sample

A good email does not need to be long. It should make it clear that you have looked at my current research and can explain how your interests connect to it. Generic emails sent to many supervisors are usually not competitive.

The strongest enquiries usually come from applicants who can say something concrete, such as:

> I am interested in collective robotics, multi-robot learning, or embodied autonomy, and I would like to develop a PhD topic around [specific problem]. My previous work involved [specific method or system], and I think this background could fit with your work on [specific project or research direction].

If your background is mostly in another area, that is not necessarily a problem. Robotics PhD projects often need people with strengths in machine learning, computer vision, control, optimisation, software systems, or hardware. What matters is whether there is a plausible path from your current skills to the proposed research.

I also cannot usually create a funded PhD position on short notice. If there is no current funding available, I may still be able to discuss a possible application if you are okay to start without funding, suggest a better-fitting topic, or point you to relevant advertised opportunities. But for international applicants in particular, the process takes time and competition for funding is serious.

Before writing, please also check the UNSW Higher Degree Research application and scholarship pages, since the formal requirements, deadlines, and funding schemes can change. The university's PhD entry requirements and scholarship requirements are set centrally, not by me or any individual supervisor.
